Human-centered AI/ML - Case of a toxicity-detecting machine learning model

How can we evaluate an AI/ML model beyond technical metrics such as precision, recall, and so on? How can we provide insights about how this model works in a real-world sociotechnical setting, especially to UX professionals who design applications on top of such models? A set of research projects will focus on this topic with respect to a toxicity-detecting ML model - Perspective.

  • We conducted a research study with the goal of testing Perspective's transferability. Perspective was largely trained on one source of data, but is used in different online, social platforms. So how well does Perspective transfer what it has learned to other platforms? We gathered data (online comments) from 3 platforms to compare human and machine ratings of toxicity. Our human-centered evaluation shows 1) us whether or not users align with the model and 2) how Perspective behaves in different social platforms. More details here.

Past Projects

  • HCI & Journalism, HCDE, UW

    I participated in a Directed Research Group associated with the Center for an Informed Public, UW. The goal was to analyze data from semi-structured interviews of national and international journalists working on online misinformation and disinformation. We did open coding of the data followed by a thorough qualitative analysis using affinity diagramming. I wrote up memos for the challenges faced by journalists (particularly in investigating data on social media platforms) and design implications, and contributed to publication efforts.

  • Virtual Reality (VR) to support transformative narrative experiences, Informatics, UC Irvine

    Can VR support role-playing? We designed and developed an interactive storytelling experience based on an original theatrical musical. Our design, informed by performing arts theories, offers opportunties for the interactor to take up a role. We conducted a formative, qualitative, study with 12 participants which included observations and interviews. We studied how and when the design enables or hinders participants role-playing experiences, and present design implications to explicitly support transformative, role-playing experiences.

  • Visualizing Algorithm Selection in Social Media, Informatics, UC Irvine

    Are users aware they are in algorithmically created filter bubbles / echo chambers? Can this awareness be the first step towards breaking these silos? If so, how can they attain it in context, while using the social media platform? This pilot study explores these questions using an experimental study design. A visualization that depicts how tweets of one opinion/topic/theme are populated by the algorithm serves as the experimental condition. This work reveals how participants develop different, subjective interpretations of the visualization and its purpose and are guided by strong personal values and convictions.
